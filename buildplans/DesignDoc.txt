D&D Party Management Simulation
Design Document – Final Draft (Updated)

Game Overview
A high-level management simulation game based on OD&D/AD&D rules. Players oversee a pool of adventurers and are tasked with keeping them alive, funded, and progressing through dungeons while developing supporting town infrastructure. There is no "win" state — the world persists and evolves, with a long-term dream of procedural dungeons forming from retired adventurers.

Visual Style & User Interface
Minimalist 1-bit (black and white) sprites and graphics


Each adventurer is represented by a 1-bit sprite themed by class


Color customization for adventurers (via picker or preset colors)


Clean 2D UI, focused on functionality and legibility


Dungeon lairs displayed as minimalist expanding network graphs


Hover to reveal detailed info on dungeon nodes


Thematic news feed (e.g., town crier scroll) for event notifications


Reports and stats displayed via MS-DOS-style lists or compact cards


Expedition reports styled like Paradox-style ledgers with large fonts



Core Gameplay Loop
Choose adventurers from a fixed pool of 20


Create parties (2–9 members each; multiple parties supported)


Assign goals: treasure, XP, clearing, or exploration


Send them on expeditions (minimal direct control)


Resolve outcomes via simulations and reports


Use returns to upgrade adventurers or invest in town facilities



Player Decisions
Form optimal parties with complementary skills


Choose expedition targets and goals


Decide when to invest in town roles or expeditions


Manage gold for upkeep, gear, and facility development


Assign high-level characters to town roles as infrastructure evolves



Game Systems
Adventurers
20 pre-generated characters


No player-created adventurers


Adventurer stats:


Class (Fighter, Cleric, Magic-User, Elf, Dwarf, Hobbit)


Level (start at 1)


XP (1gp = 1xp; monsters grant 100xp/HD)


HP, Equipment, Money, Spells (for spellcasters)


Demi-humans:


Elves are dual-class F/MU


Racial level caps, but unique advantages


Character Advancement
Automatic level-ups per OD&D XP thresholds


Spells randomly acquired per level-up


Followers gained automatically at high levels


Hit points, saving throws, and attack bonuses improve per OD&D


Character Death & Recovery
Death is permanent unless resurrection is available


Resurrection: Requires high-level cleric + proper temple staffing


Cost follows OD&D rules


Non-resurrected adventurers are interred via town Temple system


Fail states include death, petrification, charm, etc.



Parties
Create/edit multiple adventuring parties (2–9 members)


Success affected by party composition


Treasure split options: equal share, by level, or other methods



Time Management
Dungeon expeditions: 1-week cycle (3 prep, 1 delve, 3 return)


Healing: 2 days per HP


Recruitment: 1 week


Game is turn-based with auto-progression (like OOTP)


Future optional real-time bonus mode: real-time weekly delves



Economy & Risk Management
Monthly upkeep: 1% of XP in gold


Key risk loop:


Treasure vs. exploration


Upgrades vs. savings


Town support vs. dungeon delving


Gold uses:


Buying gear


Upkeep


Hiring henchmen (1d6 × 100 gp/week)


(Future) Carousing



Dungeon Structure
Procedurally generated lair network


Each lair:


Dominant monster type


Exploration and danger level


Treasure level


Connections to deeper areas


Travel is node-to-node, affected by exploration and encumbrance


Example dungeon levels and monster types:


Level 1: Kobold, Goblin, Skeleton, Orc


Level 2: Hobgoblin, Zombie, Theurgist, Ghoul


...


Level 6: Dragon, Giant, Vampire, Demon Lord



Encounter System (Simulated)
Encounters checked every 10 in-game minutes


Resolution steps:


Surprise roll


Initiative


Action choice: fight, flee, parlay


Simulation of OD&D-style combat


Players observe, not direct, combat



Parlay System
2d6 Reaction Table:


2: Fight


3–5: Fight or flee


6–8: Uncertain


9–11: Join (roll loyalty)


12: Join (loyalty +2)



Expedition Reporting System
Detailed logs with options for quick or comprehensive review


Logs include:


Encounter rolls/results


Damage, spells, deaths


Treasure and XP earned


Notifications for key events


Persistent archive for long-term tracking



Dungeon Expedition Goals
Treasure Hunting – Prioritize loot, leave when full


Clearing – Eradicate/diplomatically remove monsters


XP Gain – Seek easy fights and treasure


Exploration – Reveal more dungeon structure



Town Development
Characters can be assigned to infrastructure (non-adventuring)


Examples:


Temple – Clerics (Lvl 2+) provide healing; high-levels can resurrect


Wizard Tower – Spell research and item crafting


Fighter Strongholds/Baronies – Tax income, attract retainers


Facility roster still in development; each class to have contributions at lower and name levels



Magic & Spells
Magic-users receive randomly assigned spells


Strategic management of party synergy is essential


No manual spell choice: players adapt to randomness



Gameplay Milestones
First treasure returned


First monster defeated


Lair cleared


New dungeon area discovered


100% lair exploration


Facility unlocked or constructed
